From Weimar to Zürich: How the Bauhaus Became Swiss

May 2026 · AP Literature Research Presentation
Draft — in progress

The Swiss International Style did not appear out of nowhere in the 1950s. When the Bauhaus closed in 1933, the people who built it had to go somewhere. A lot of them ended up in Switzerland. The Swiss Style is not a separate tradition. It is the Bauhaus, translated by the same people, in a country where the work could continue.

Most design history treats the two movements like a sequence. Bauhaus ends in 1933, Swiss Style begins in the 1950s, and the twenty years in between are a blank. What actually happened in those twenty years was emigration. Max Bill went to Zürich. Herbert Bayer went to the United States but his typography kept traveling. Paul Klee, who had been teaching color theory at the Bauhaus in Dessau, planted ideas that turned up in Swiss grid work decades later. The Bauhaus did not end. It moved.

Max Bill and the direct line

The clearest evidence is Max Bill. He studied at the Dessau Bauhaus under Gropius from 1927 to 1929. What he learned there was the school's basic position: form should follow function, design is not separate from fine art, typography is architecture. He went back to Switzerland and spent the next thirty years building institutions that carried this thinking on. The Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm, where he was rector, was a Bauhaus successor in everything but name.

Bill's own graphic work — his posters, his magazine layouts, his type — is the Bauhaus vocabulary translated into Swiss. The grid is tighter. The sans-serif is pushed further. There is less color. But the logic is the same logic he learned in Dessau. Make the structure of the page visible. Let the grid do the work. Distrust ornament.

What emigration changed

Switzerland was not Germany. Political neutrality, precision manufacturing culture, and the fact that anything published had to work in German, French, and Italian at once — these pushed the emigrant designers toward something more systematic than what they had practiced at the Bauhaus. The grid was not an aesthetic preference anymore. It was a practical answer to a real publishing problem.

This is where the Swiss Style diverges from the Bauhaus while still being continuous with it. The Bauhaus produced radical experiments: asymmetric layouts, experimental typefaces, collage. The Swiss Style produced a method. Same underlying logic, but turned into a system you could teach and reproduce. What the emigrant designers built in Zürich and Basel was a way to apply Bauhaus thinking at the scale of a country.

Personal · Audio essay

Why I Applied to UWC-USA

May 2026 · NYT Student Podcast Challenge

There is a specific quality of light in Mendocino in the late afternoon, after the fog burns off and the sun gets low over the water. It comes through the cypress trees in long flat lines. My dad built our house here. It is where I have lived my whole life. Leaving is not a small thing.

I applied to UWC-USA in the fall. The real decision, not the application, happened in a hotel room on a trip with my friend Eli. I sat with it for a while. I tend to work through things slowly, all the way through. UWC means leaving the ocean. It means leaving the town I know by name and by light. It means going to New Mexico, which is nothing like the California coast, and spending two years on a campus with students from sixty countries.

The question was never whether UWC was a good opportunity. It is. The question was what I would be giving up, and whether the trade was honest. You can know something is right and still feel the weight of what it costs you.

What decided it was the long arc. I want to study civil engineering at ETH Zurich. I want to build infrastructure — bridges, transit, the things that move people and water through physical space, not software. For that I need German at C1. I need the IB. I need to have lived in Europe, or somewhere that runs on European terms. UWC gets me all three. Mendocino, as much as I love it, does not point toward Zürich.

So I said yes. I leave in fall 2026. The ocean will still be here when I come back.

On Making Detention

May 2026 · In production
Draft — written during production

Seraphine and I wrote Detention in the grammar of Wes Anderson, which means we wrote it under a lot of constraints. Every shot is centered. Every cut is motivated. The actors do not move unless the script says they move. The blocking is architectural. The delivery is dry.

What Anderson does, and what we are trying to do, is find the comedy in the gap between what a situation demands emotionally and how the characters actually respond to it. Detention is a context that should produce anxiety. Or frustration. Or boredom. The joke is that the characters are precise about it. They treat it like a meeting agenda. Flat affect is not the absence of feeling. It is a specific register.

Filming in this style is harder than it looks. The symmetry requirement means every location has to be scouted differently. You are not looking for interesting light. You are looking for a room that will hold a centered frame without looking accidental. We spent more time in pre-production than I expected, doing floor-plan sketches of the classroom before we touched a camera.

The casting matters more than usual. Anderson's humor depends on actors who commit totally to the deadpan. A single moment of ironic distance — an actor who winks at the camera with their eyes — breaks the whole logic. We are still in production as I write this. More notes after we wrap.

On design

My taste runs Swiss and Bauhaus. Grid, restraint, warm color, nothing decorative that isn't doing work. The research project I'm doing this spring is about how Bauhaus designers literally moved to Switzerland in the 30s and brought the school's logic with them. Design as a history, not a toolkit.
