From Weimar to Zürich: How the Bauhaus Became Swiss

May 2026 · AP Literature Research Presentation
Draft — in progress

The Swiss International Style did not arrive fully formed from the Alps. It came in suitcases — the luggage of designers who left Germany as the Bauhaus closed and the political situation made their work impossible. The argument is simple: the Swiss Style is not a separate tradition. It is the Bauhaus, translated by the people who built it and transplanted to a country that had the stability to let it mature.

Most design history treats the two movements as sequential — Bauhaus ends in 1933, Swiss Style begins in the 1950s — as though there were a twenty-year gap during which nothing happened. What actually happened was emigration. Max Bill arrived in Zürich. Herbert Bayer went to the United States but his typography kept traveling. Paul Klee, already at the Bauhaus in Dessau, had been teaching color theory that would resurface in Swiss grid work decades later. The Bauhaus did not end. It relocated.

Max Bill and the direct line

The clearest evidence is Max Bill. He studied at the Dessau Bauhaus under Gropius from 1927 to 1929, absorbing the school's core tenet — that form should follow function, that design and fine art were not separate disciplines, that typography was architecture. He returned to Switzerland and spent the next three decades building institutions that would carry these ideas forward: the Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm, where he served as rector, was a direct institutional successor to the Bauhaus in everything but name.

Bill's own graphic work — his exhibition posters, his magazine layouts, his typography — shows the Bauhaus vocabulary translated into the Swiss idiom. The grid is more rigid. The sans-serif is pushed further. The color is reduced. But the logic is the same logic he learned in Dessau: make the structure of the page visible; let the grid do the work; distrust ornament.

What emigration changed

Switzerland was not Germany. The Swiss context — political neutrality, precision manufacturing culture, multilingual publishing needs — pushed the emigrant designers toward something more systematic than what they had practiced at the Bauhaus. The need to design for German, French, and Italian simultaneously demanded a typography that worked across languages without privileging any one of them. The grid became more than an aesthetic choice. It became a practical solution.

This is where the Swiss Style diverged from its Bauhaus origins while remaining continuous with them. The Bauhaus had produced radical experimentation — asymmetric layouts, experimental typefaces, collage. The Swiss Style produced a method. The same underlying logic, but systematized, teachable, reproducible. What the emigrant designers built in Zürich and Basel was a way to apply Bauhaus thinking at scale.

Why I Applied to UWC-USA

May 2026 · NYT Student Podcast Challenge

There's a specific quality of light in Mendocino in the late afternoon, when the fog has burned off and the sun is low over the water. It comes through the cypress trees in long flat lines. My dad built our house here. It's where I've lived my whole life. Leaving is not a small thing.

I applied to UWC-USA in the fall. The decision — the real decision, not the application — happened in a hotel room on a trip with my close friend Eli. I remember sitting quietly, working through it the way I tend to work through things: all the way through, not quickly. UWC means leaving the ocean. It means leaving the town I know by name and by light. It means going to New Mexico, which is nothing like the California coast, and spending two years on a campus with students from sixty countries preparing for university in Europe.

The tension wasn't about whether UWC was a good opportunity. It clearly is. The tension was about what I'd be giving up, and whether the trade was honest. You can know something is right and still feel the weight of what it costs.

What decided it for me was the long arc. I want to study civil engineering at ETH Zurich. I want to build infrastructure — not software, not systems that live in computers, but things that move people and water and weight through physical space. To do that I need German at C1. I need the IB. I need to have lived in Europe, or somewhere that functions on European terms. UWC gives me all of that. Mendocino — as much as I love it, as much as it's made me who I am — doesn't point toward Zurich.

So I said yes. I'm leaving in fall 2026. The ocean will still be there when I come back.

On Making Detention

May 2026 · In production
Draft — written during production

Seraphine and I wrote Detention in the grammar of Wes Anderson, which means we wrote it in the grammar of constraint. Every shot is centered. Every cut is motivated. The actors don't move unless the script says they move. The blocking is architectural. The delivery is dry.

The thing Anderson does — the thing we're trying to do — is find the comedy in the gap between what a situation demands emotionally and how the characters actually respond to it. Detention is a context that should produce anxiety, frustration, boredom. The joke is that the characters are precise about it. They treat it the way you'd treat a meeting agenda. Flat affect is not absence of feeling. It's a specific register.

Filming in this style is harder than it looks. The symmetry requirement means every location has to be scouted differently. You're not looking for interesting light or character; you're looking for a room that will hold a centered frame without reading as accidental. We spent more time in pre-production than I expected, doing floor-plan sketches of the classroom before we touched a camera.

The casting matters more than usual too. Anderson's humor depends on actors who commit totally to the deadpan. A single moment of ironic distance — an actor who winks at the camera with their eyes — breaks the whole logic. We're still in production as I write this. More notes when we wrap.

From a small town on the California coast.

I grew up in Mendocino, California — a town of about a thousand people on a bluff above the Pacific. My dad, Pablo, built our house by hand. The ocean is a constant presence, and it's shaped how I think about what endures and what doesn't. I'm Italian and American by citizenship, and the European thread has always been there — in my family, in the language I'm working toward, in the architecture and design I find myself drawn to.

I'm a junior at Mendocino High School. In fall 2026 I'm leaving for UWC-USA in Montezuma, New Mexico to start the IB Diploma Programme — a two-year high school with students from sixty countries, the kind of education that points toward the world I want to live in. After that: ETH Zurich, civil engineering, and a career building physical infrastructure in Europe. Not software. Bridges, transit systems, the things that move people through cities.

In the meantime I make films, run a radio show, edit video professionally, and write code when something I need doesn't exist yet. I think the creative and technical work feed each other more than most people expect — directing a short film and building an app require the same thing: deciding what to leave out.

Interests

Formula 1 and motorsports, soccer, tennis, travel, the German language and European urbanism, Swiss graphic design, film grammar, the mechanics of a well-run radio show, how cities are actually built.

On design

My aesthetic runs Swiss/Bauhaus — grid, restraint, warm color, no decoration that isn't doing work. The AP Lit paper I'm writing this spring is about exactly this: how Bauhaus designers physically emigrated to Switzerland and seeded what became the Swiss International Style. I'm interested in design as a discipline with a history, not a toolkit.
